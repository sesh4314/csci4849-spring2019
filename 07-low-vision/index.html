Slide 7

What is low vision anyway?

  • An estimated 253 million people live with vision impairment: 36 million (14%) are blind and 217 million (85%) have moderate to severe vision impairment
  • 81% of people who are blind or have moderate or severe vision impairment are aged 50 years and above
  • Data from World Health Organization

Slide 8

How low vision users are different

  • Typically use magnification and high-contrast mode
  • Even when screen readers could be more efficient
  • For computer stuff and everyday materials (print etc.)
  • May not have blindness training skills (Braille, white cane, screen readers)
  • Can be progressive (e.g. Usher Syndrome)
  • Comorbid conditions: deafness (deafblind), cognitive, motor, aging

Slide 12

Types of challenges

  • Seeing fine details
  • Differentiating colors or patterns
  • Visual comparisons (when zoomed in)
  • Peripheral vision and group awareness
  • Environmental effects: glare, brightness, darkness

As always, we should think about activities and how they might be impeded. For example, working in the kitchen can involve distance vision, or comparing multiple items side by side.

Slide 13

What is it like using a magnifier?

  • Subtle visual details can be blown out
  • Very little information on the screen at one time
  • Requires lots of scrolling
Slide 14

Magnification or screen reader?

  • Learning to use a screen reader is difficult
  • People will often hold onto their visual reading ability, even when performance is worse
  • Magnification is often personalized: magnification level, colors

The point here is that we can't assume that a person will use the most efficient method.

    Slide 23

    Color blindness

    • Affects about 10% of men; fewer women
    • Difficulty distinguishing colors
    • Fits into several main categories, but individual differences remain

    Color blind people don't actually see the world in black and white. Instead, they tend to see colors that most people recognize as different colors, as a single color.

    Slide 24

      The important point is not that color blind people have weird vision. The point is that this can affect what people are trying to do, like identifying a specific bird, or picking a ripe tomato.

      Images from Bored Panda. Left: 4 copies of a photo showing three parrots; showing normal vision, deuteranomaly, protanopia, and tritanopia. Right: 4 copies of an image of tomatoes with the same 4 conditions.

      Slide 25

      Designing for color blindness

      • Don't use color alone to distinguish items
      • Instead, combine with shape, texture, or some other factor
      • Avoid a special color blind friendly mode; make it work out of the box

      It is possible to test for the different types of color blindness and accommodate them, but some users might still be unable to use your product due to their unique color vision. It is better just to have a backup way of differentiating items.

      Image: Trello offers color blind friendly mode which changes the visual texture of labels. The only thing they could do better would be to make this by default.

      Slide 29

      Deafblindness

      • Combine some vision loss with some hearing loss
      • Between 70,000 and 100,00 in the US
      • About 50% have a condition called Usher Syndrome
      • Born deaf, lose vision over time (especially peripheral vision)
      • May grow up speaking sign languages like ASL; may learn tactile sign language and Braille
      • Typically use Braille for computer access

      Link: Color Blindness FAQ from the American Association of the Deaf-Blind

        Some impairing situations

        • Bright lights (e.g. sun)
        • Dark rooms
        • Interacting at a distance
        • Reduced visual attention may have similar effects (e.g. using tech while driving)
        Slide 34

        How to handle low vision on the web

        • Offer font size buttons
        • Offer a high-contrast or large print stylesheet
        • Users may adopt their own custom stylesheet
        Slide 35

        Takeaways

        • Many different forms of vision impairment
        • So, take a functional approach
        • Give users control over presentation
        • Use color and contrast wisely
        • Consider opportunities to make the outside world more accessible too
